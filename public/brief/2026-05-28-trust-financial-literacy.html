Trust, Financial Literacy, and Financial Behaviors: Shaping Retirement Security

Bottom Line

A new NBER working paper by Maya Haran Rosen, Annamaria Lusardi (Stanford SIEPR), and Olivia S. Mitchell demonstrates that financial literacy's impact on retirement security is deeply mediated by trust. Crucially, the study reveals stark racial disparities: trust in financial institutions boosts retirement readiness for White respondents, but fails to yield the same benefits for Black and Hispanic households, who see reduced wealth accumulation linked to alternative trust in government programs.

Question

How does institutional trust interact with financial literacy to influence retirement preparation, and do these effects differ across racial and ethnic lines?

Key Findings

  • Trust Types Matter: Trust in Financial Institutions correlates with positive retirement security behaviors, whereas Trust in Government Programs does not.
  • Racial Disparities: The positive link between financial institutional trust and retirement outcomes is present for White respondents, but absent for Black and Hispanic respondents.
  • Wealth Accumulation Drag: Among minority households, placing trust primarily in government programs is associated with reduced stockholding and overall lower wealth accumulation.

Analysis

This paper is highly relevant for policymakers and financial educators because it isolates a "trust gap" that limits the efficacy of financial literacy interventions. If financial capability programs simply impart knowledge without addressing systemic distrust in banks and financial markets—particularly among minority populations—they may fail to close the racial wealth gap. The data suggests that financial education must be coupled with institutional reforms that earn trust from marginalized communities.

What Would Change This View

  • Subsequent research isolating the causal direction (e.g., does lack of wealth cause lack of trust, or vice versa?).
  • Longitudinal studies tracking whether newly introduced inclusive banking initiatives repair this trust deficit over time.

Follow-Up Questions

  • How can financial education curriculums explicitly address and navigate historical distrust of financial institutions among minority groups?
  • Are there specific consumer protection policies (via CFPB) that could effectively bridge this institutional trust gap?
