# The Spillover Effect: How K-12 Financial Education Impacts Parents

## Bottom Line

Financial education delivered to high school students does not just improve the financial trajectory of the student—it creates a measurable, empirical "spillover effect" that actively improves the financial behavior of their parents.

## Question

Does K-12 financial education only benefit the students in the classroom, or does it have a wider economic impact?

## Key Findings

- **The Spillover Effect:** Research by Veronica Frisancho Robles proves that high school financial education has "spillover effects" that carry over to parents' financial knowledge and behavior.
- **Credit Score Improvements:** Among parents from poorer households, the treatment resulted in a 5% increase in credit scores and a 26% reduction in default probability. 
- **Lasting Impact:** The behavioral improvements in the parents were observable up to three years after the student received the education.
- **Demographic Nuance:** The treatment had stronger effects among the parents of daughters, who experienced a significant 6.7% increase in their credit score.
- **Award-Winning Validation:** This research won the "Best Academic Project or Paper" at the Money Awareness and Inclusion Awards (MAIA), and has been publicly praised by Dr. Annamaria Lusardi of Stanford SIEPR as "some of the most compelling work being done in this field."

## Analysis

This research provides a massive piece of ammunition for advocates lobbying state legislatures. The typical argument against mandates is that they are too expensive or only help kids far in the future. Frisancho's data proves that K-12 mandates yield an *immediate* economic return for the state by lowering adult default rates and raising adult credit scores in the present day.
