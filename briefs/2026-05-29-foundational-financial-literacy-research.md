# Foundational Research in Financial Literacy and Education Policy

## Bottom Line

The field of financial literacy is anchored by a few foundational studies that transition it from an abstract idea to a measurable economic imperative. The research proves a distinct dichotomy: while adult financial capability remains deeply stagnant (and is actively declining for Gen Z), systemic policy interventions—specifically K-12 mandates and active-learning/gamified interventions—produce empirically verifiable improvements in wealth outcomes. The cost of illiteracy is a quantifiable "Fragility Tax."

## Question

What is the most important research in the history of the financial education and policy fields?

## Key Findings

- **The Big Three Questions:** Annamaria Lusardi and Olivia Mitchell established the global standard for measuring financial literacy (interest compounding, inflation, and risk diversification), proving that illiteracy is widespread and directly correlated with wealth inequality.
- **The Stagnation Dichotomy:** The FINRA National Financial Capability Study confirms that adult financial literacy has hovered around 49%, while Gen Z literacy has dropped to roughly 38%.
- **The Policy Response:** NGPF tracking shows a surging legislative response, with 25 states now mandating personal finance courses for K-12 graduation.
- **Intervention Efficacy:** NBER meta-analyses demonstrate that financial education works best when it utilizes active learning (gamification, storytelling) and is mandated at the systemic (K-12) level.
- **The Fragility Tax:** Individuals lacking fundamental financial knowledge pay higher fees, accrue more debt, borrow at higher rates, and systematically under-save, effectively paying a tax for their illiteracy.

## Analysis

The history of financial education research can be split into two eras: pre-measurement and post-measurement. 

Before Lusardi and Mitchell introduced the "Big Three" questions, financial education was largely qualitative and disjointed. Their work allowed economists to treat financial literacy as a form of human capital. Once measurable, it became clear that illiteracy operates as a regressive "Fragility Tax" on households.

Currently, the field is defined by a massive divergence. FINRA and TIAA Institute data show a failure of ad-hoc adult education, evidenced by stagnant capability scores and Gen Z's alarming baseline. Conversely, Stanford SIEPR, NBER, and NGPF data validate the current policy surge: universal K-12 mandates work, particularly when paired with high-engagement, active-learning modalities like 2-minute storytelling and gamification.

## What Would Change This View

- Longitudinal data showing that K-12 mandates fail to produce better financial outcomes for students once they reach their mid-20s.
- Evidence that point-of-sale "just-in-time" AI interventions completely negate the need for foundational human financial literacy.
